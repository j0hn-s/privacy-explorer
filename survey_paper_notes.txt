Abstract
Privacy Enhancing Technologies have become central to contemporary data sharing and analytics strategies across sensitive domains, including finance, healthcare, and government. Over the past decade, rapid advances in cryptography, systems design, and machine learning have expanded the PET landscape, yet the literature remains fragmented. Existing surveys predominantly focus on individual techniques, narrow threat models, or sector specific use cases, offering limited guidance on how PETs are combined, parameterised, and assessed in real deployments. We address this gap by providing a recent, implementation centred synthesis of PETs organised into broad algorithmic and architectural typologies. Rather than cataloguing mechanisms, we analyse how privacy guarantees are realised in practice through compositional design choices, trust assumptions, performance constraints, and assurance artefacts. Drawing on peer reviewed research, standards activity, and documented deployments, we identify cross cutting challenges that recur across PET families, including privacy-utility trade offs, scalability limits, integration risk, and verification gaps. We introduce a unifying logic model that reframes PET adoption as an architectural and evidentiary problem, enabling systematic comparison across sectors and analytical purposes. By abstracting common deployment patterns and failure modes, the survey moves beyond static taxonomies towards a framework for evidence driven reasoning about PET combinations, maturity, and risk, providing a foundation for more coherent and dependable privacy preserving system design.

1. Introduction
A growing availability of sensitive data in domains such as finance, healthcare, and governance has catalysed interest in Privacy-Enhancing Technologies (PETs), alongside a growing demand to link and analyse distributed datasets to uncover new patterns and value without undermining privacy, confidentiality and trust (OECD 2023; Royal Society, 2023). PETs are a group of technical tools that enable data analytics and sharing while protecting individual privacy and are typically classified into broad categories based on how they protect data (Ameur & Bouzefrane, 2023).   PETs are conventionally grouped by whether they protect inputs during processing or outputs at release. This framing is useful for procurement and programme design because it can help guide practical decisions around deployment architecture and operational controls, which shape implementation cost and project setup.

In this survey, we retain the input-output distinction as a risk lens but organise techniques into algorithmic versus architectural typologies to better support an implementation-centred discussion of how privacy guarantees are realised and combined in practice.  For instance, a statistics agency may keep sensitive records inside a controlled compute environment, an architectural control shifting trust, key management and attestation mechanisms, while applying algorithmic controls when releasing aggregates. The input and output lens names these risk surfaces whilst the algorithmic and architectural typology maps them to the corresponding engineering work and auditable evidence.

The two proposed typologies encompass the following technologies and techniques: 
I.	Algorithmic PETs- privacy-preserving algorithms rooted in cryptography and formal privacy definitions including secure multi-party computation (SMPC), homomorphic encryption (HE), differential privacy (DP), synthetic data and zero-knowledge proofs (ZKPs).
II.	Architectural PETs- system-level methods that aim to prevent exposure of sensitive data by design, such as federated learning (FL) and trusted execution environments (TEEs).

In practice, deployments often span both across both typologies. We use the split to clarify which assurances attach to the system boundary versus the privacy mechanism. We review each typology, alongside encompassed groups of techniques, with an emphasis on recent academic literature and practical implementations, highlighting cross-cutting technical challenges and variations in adoption across different sectors. 

Crucially, our focus remains on technical and implementation aspects, drawing from peer-reviewed research and high-quality reports for an academically rigorous and industrially practical perspective. Adopting a recent, cross-cutting, implementation-centred lens, we highlight critical trade-offs, composability constraints and assurance artefacts across algorithmic and architectural PETs, bridging the gap between technique or sector-specific reviews and actionable, sector-agnostic guidance.

This survey adopts an implementation-centred perspective on PETs. We first review algorithmic and architectural PETs through the assumptions, interfaces, and evidence that shape practical privacy properties. We then examine the cross-cutting technical challenges that recur across PET families, including trade-offs in utility, performance, integration, composability, verification, and standardisation. Building on this, we compare how PETs and PET combinations are adopted across sectors through their trust assumptions, assurance posture, deployment maturity, and the standards or legal-regulatory considerations that shape whether technical guarantees can be relied upon in practice. We conclude by outlining broader limitations and future directions for more coherent, evidence-based, and dependable privacy-preserving system design.

2. PET Typologies and State-of-the-Art Techniques
 This section surveys state-of-the-art PETs through the proposed typologies, focusing on the assumptions and interfaces that determine what is protected during the computation and at release, rather than cataloguing mechanisms. To keep the review implementation-grounded, we outline parameterisation and composition choice, performance and communication constraints, and evidence needed to substantiate privacy claims. We do not cover baseline security controls, such as conventional encryption at rest or in transit, or general de-identification measures, such as pseudonymisation in depth, as practice and regulatory guidance treat these as complementary prerequisites rather than the PET mechanisms this section maps and compares. 
2.1 Algorithmic PETs
Algorithmic PETs protect data through privacy preserving transformations, ranging from cryptographic secure computation and formal privacy definitions, such as DP, to output transformation mechanisms, such as synthetic data, whose guarantees depend on explicit constraints and evaluation. Those based on cryptographic secure computation and formal privacy definitions often provide strong privacy in a provable sense but can incur performance and complexity costs (Dwork & Roth, 2014; Evans et al., 2018).

2.1.1 Secure Multi-Party Computation
SMPC refers to protocols that allow multiple parties to jointly compute a function over their private inputs without revealing those inputs to each other. Seminal work established that any function can be computed with perfect secrecy in theory (Yao, 1986; Goldreich et al., 1987). Modern research emphasises practical protocols that minimise the heavy computation and communication overhead inherent in MPC (Evans et al., 2018). For example, the SPDZ protocol, a framework for SMPC, and its variants, which predominantly consist of cryptographic primitives, allow general secure arithmetic computations with improved efficiency (Archer et al., 2018). 

Performance remains a challenge in many such applications. Basic operations in an MPC protocol can be orders of magnitude slower than their plaintext equivalents (Evans et al., 2018; Schneider, 2020).. Empirical evaluations have repeatedly demonstrated that MPC-based ML training can impose substantial computation and communication overheads, and this remains a central engineering constraint in motivating ongoing privacy-preserving ML investigations. Reported overhead varies with the model, data dimensionality, security model, and network setting, which is why evaluations are typically provided as task-specific time or communication benchmarks rather than single multiplier (Mohassel and Zhang, 2017; Mohassel and Rindal, 2018). For example, two-party logistic regression training over 70,000 samples, encompassing 15 features, for six epochs reported at 208.09MB online communication and 9.69 minutes online time over WAN (Agarwal et al., 2022). Even with optimised protocols, tasks like training logistic regression on high-dimensional data can require billions of primitive operations and tens of seconds in a local network setting (Riazi et al., 2018). 

Current research into privacy-preserving ML often centres on SMPC combined with efficiency workarounds or hybrid approaches, such as using lightweight encryption or data-oblivious algorithms, to reduce computational overhead (Mitchell et al., 2021). Ongoing efforts focus on supporting more complex analytics and reducing the round complexity of MPC protocols (Yin et al., 2021). Despite progress, MPC’s heavy resource requirements have limited its broad deployment so far to use-cases that can tolerate some latency or that involve relatively small data sizes, such as secure collaborative credit scoring among banks, or secure genomic analysis competitions, like iDASH, challenges in healthcare (Archer et al., 2018; Froelicher et al., 2021). Notably, domain-specific MPC frameworks, such as privacy-preserving genome-wide association studies or Language Integrated Query (LINQ) database querying, and cloud-based MPC services are emerging, reflecting slow but growing maturity (Archer et al., 2018; Knott et al., 2021).

2.1.2 Homomorphic Encryption
HE schemes allow computations on encrypted data such that the decrypted results match what would have been obtained on plaintext. In a fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) scheme, one can perform arbitrary computations on ciphertexts (Gentry, 2009). FHE provides strong input privacy by ensuring that data can remain encrypted throughout processing. Recent advances in FHE, including the CKKS (Cheon-Kim-Kim-Song) scheme for efficient arithmetic on encrypted real numbers, have enabled practical evaluations of ML models such as inference on neural networks with acceptable accuracy loss (Kim et al. 2018; Chillotti et al. 2020; Cheon et al. 2017; Badawi et al. 2020). Complementary secure computation work targets nonlinear bottlenecks directly. Mixed-mode MPC protocols using wavelet compressed lookup tables to accelerate common ML activation functions, for example, highlight that practical privacy-preserving ML often advances via specialised treatments of nonlinearities alongside advances in HE (Reis et al., 2025).

However, performance and resource overhead remain FHE’s biggest bottleneck. Even basic operations using state-of-the-art algorithms can be 10³-10⁴ times slower than on plaintext, and ciphertext sizes are much larger than plaintexts (Halevi & Shoup, 2020). For instance, Halevi and Shoup (2020) report that a single FHE multiplication of two numbers can be 4-5 orders of magnitude slower than an unencrypted multiplication. Recent practitioner-facing forums emphasise that closing the FHE practicality gap depends on hardware acceleration, as well as implementation and security guidance (NIST, 2025). Simultaneously, there is a growing corpus of research focused on non-interactive training and clearer workload-fit boundaries. PoPETs 2026 reports an efficient non-interactive XGBoost training system over FHE, including improved argmax machinery making operations more efficient under encryption, while a recent SoK provides a functional and cost analysis of whether, and at what cost, FHE can support AI workloads mixing linear and non-linear operations (Masalha et al., 2026; Xue et al., 2025).

Beyond algorithmic advances the practical use of FHE is increasingly shaped by practitioner and industry constraints, such as security model fit, parameterisation, and hardware co-design. The NIST WPEC 2024 FHE session introduces these implementation concerns, discussing: 
I.	How FHE security guarantees map to application settings;
II.	Practical acceleration pathways, such as FPGA-based approaches;
III.	Empirical performance for CKKS in encrypted training and inference, and;
IV.	Security and implementation guidance aimed at deployers.

In parallel IBM Research presents FHE as addressing the confidentiality gap during processing and has built toolchains and demonstrations for AI on encrypted data, alongside substantial publications focused on FHE, signalling an increased research, engineering and investment effort into workload-drive optimisation and deployment maturity (IBM 2024; IBM, 2026). Some exploratory avenues of research identify quantum workflows as a potential mechanism to reduce some of this computational burden in privacy preserving analytics. NQCC case studies cite privacy sensitive federated quantum learning in biomedical and genomic research contexts as an emerging direction but do not yet report measurable reductions in classical FHE overhead (NQCC, 2025a; NQCC, 2025b). 
 To summarise, supporting general AI workloads under FHE remains costly and requires highly specialised designs, particularly for large-scale or real-time analytics. Recent research and engineering efforts are addressing this via hardware acceleration and more efficient, or ‘levelled’, HE that avoids expensive bootstrapping when only specific computations are required (Wang et al., 2022; Dathathri et al., 2020). At present, FHE is practical mainly for relatively small computations, including evaluating fixed ML models on a handful of inputs, or summing encrypted values from multiple sources, and often in combination with other techniques. For example, a system might use FHE to aggregate distributed statistics while relying on faster, local processing for less sensitive operations: a pattern seen in some recent privacy-preserving data sharing prototypes (Weerakkody et al., 2021). The long-term vision is that advancing algorithms and specialised hardware will make FHE more broadly feasible (Moore, 2026), and industry  has begun exploring early cloud-hosted prototyping offerings for encrypted analytics. IBM’s FHE cloud service HE4Cloud, built on HElayers SDK to support privacy-preserving ML inference, and limited forms of training, serves as an early example of such application, with public case-study reporting in financial services (IBM, 2025; IBM Research, 2022; IBM, 2020).  
2.1.3 Differential Privacy
DP is a mathematically rigorous framework that provides provable privacy guarantees by bounding how much an algorithm’s output can change when any single individual’s data is included or excluded, thereby limiting what can be inferred about that individual from the released result (Dwork et al., 2006; Dwork & Roth, 2014). A randomised algorithm is ‘ε-differentially private’ if changing a single person’s data causes, at most, a small change in the probability distribution of outputs, with Epsilon (ε) being the upper bound on this information leakage. In practice, DP is often implemented by adding statistical noise to query results, model parameters, or training processes. For example, the Laplace mechanism, used for numerical queries, or the Exponential mechanism, for categorical outputs, inject noise such that each query’s sensitivity to any one record is masked within a probabilistic uncertainty of scale determined by ε. 

DP has been widely studied and has been successfully employed as a statistical disclosure control for initiatives including government data releases (Garfinkel et al., 2018). A high-profile deployment is the U.S. Census Bureau’s 2020 Census Disclosure Avoidance System, which applies DP by injecting carefully calibrated noise into published tabulations to mask information about individuals and reduce re-identification risk (U.S. Census Bureau, 2021). However, independent evaluation of the 2020 DAS for redistricting finds systematic undercounting in mixed-races and mixed-partisan precincts with unpredictable racial and partisan biases and argues that the post-processing required for usability makes it difficult to balance accuracy against respondent privacy in this setting (Kenny et al., 2021). A separate critical comparison of the 2010 swapping-based disclosure limitation and the 2020 DP approach argues that DP can both degrade data quality and fail to offer meaningful privacy guarantees for sparse tables, concluding that it was neither more private nor as accurate as the prior approach for protecting the most vulnerable respondents. 

Beyond official statistics, DP has been deployed in consumer telemetry; largely through local DP mechanisms. Examples include Google’s RAPPOR and Apple’s DP telemetry measurements, as well as Google’s Privacy Sandbox measurement APIs which apply DP to continuous advertising and usage telemetry (Erlingsson et al., 2014; Tang et al., 2017; Google, 2024). These examples illustrate how DP can be operationalised for population-level statistics and show how it can be used as a mechanism to satisfy formal privacy guarantees (Ghazi et al., 2025).

A major challenge for DP is the trade-off between privacy and accuracy. DP introduces a quantifiable privacy-utility trade-off. A smaller ε means more noise and thus lower fidelity in the output data, providing a higher degree of privacy. Recent studies have shown that for complex data analytics and ML, achieving strong privacy often comes at the cost of unacceptable accuracy loss, whereas settings that preserve accuracy typically use relatively large ε values that provide weak privacy guarantees (Jayaraman & Evans, 2019). This highlights a gap between formal DP guarantees and effective privacy against realistic adversaries.

Techniques like privacy accounting and advanced DP mechanisms have improved the analysis of cumulative privacy loss in complex workflows (Mironov, 2017; Abadi et al., 2016). Additionally, local differential privacy (LDP) has emerged for decentralised data collection, allowing users to perturb data on their device before sharing, deployed by companies for telemetry collection (Erlingsson et al., 2014; Tang et al., 2017). DP methods generally provide strong output privacy, often at the cost of some accuracy or detail. Recent research focuses on mitigating this cost by contextual optimisation. For example, using partitioning and post-processing to concentrate noise on less important outputs or using ML to post-correct DP outputs (Wang et al., 2019; McKenna et al., 2021). 

Notwithstanding, DP works best for queries that can tolerate aggregated noise. For high-dimensional data or complex queries, the amount of noise needed may render results useless (Stadler et al., 2022). Determining appropriate ε values for a given application remains partly an open question beyond pure theory, often requiring policy input and user studies (Bambauer et al., 2021). Nevertheless, DP is increasingly being incorporated into data analysis tools, such as Google’s open-source DP libraries or projects leveraging OpenDP, such as Microsoft’s SmartNoise, and recommended by regulators for privacy compliance in data sharing underscoring its importance as a foundational PET (NIST, 2023; ICO, 2023; OECD, 2023).
2.1.5 A Note on Output Control and Statistical Disclosure Limitation
Although ‘anonymisation’, masking and related disclosure limitation techniques are sometimes discussed as a distinct PET family, we posit that they are best understood as algorithmic output-control strategies whose risk profile depends on the underlying assumptions and release interface. Classical syntactic models, like k-anonymity, ℓ‑diversity and t-closeness, aim to make records indistinguishable on quasi-identifiers via suppression, generalisation or perturbation, but a large body of work shows these methods can fail against linkage and auxiliary-information attacks, especially in high-dimensional settings (Sweeney, 2002; Machanavajjhala et al., 2007; Li et al., 2007; Narayanan & Shmatikov, 2008; Gymrek et al., 2013). Similar residual risk has also been demonstrated for anonymised text under ML-based re-identification attacks (Manzanares Salor et al., 2024).

Regulatory guidance reiterates that pseudonymisation remains personal data and should not be conflated with anonymisation for compliance purposes; identifier-removal approaches often do not take data out of scope (ICO, n.d.). This has also motivated prototype risk-assessment tooling, such as National Health Service (NHS) England’s Privacy FingerPrint (PrivFp) proof-of-concept aimed at producing privacy risk scores for unrestructured clinical text to help triage where additional mitigations may be required; although it remains a demonstration system (NHS, n.d.).

The core empirical challenge remains that rich datasets tend to contain singular patterns which often consist of seemingly innocuous combinations of attributes that can be uniquely identifying (Sweeny, 2002; Stadler et al., 2022; Rocher et al., 2019). As a result, contemporary best practices have shifted away from one-off static releases toward either: 
I.	DP-guided statistical releases and privacy-preserving publication frameworks, or;
II.	Restricted query interfaces that make privacy loss explicit and auditable over time (Garfinkel et al., 2018; Gaboardi et al., 2020; Snoke et al., 2018; NIST, 2025).

A recent national-statistics example of such layered output controls is ONS Census 2021, which combined targeted record swapping, cell-key perturbation, review of overall table design and disclosure rules to reduce identification and differencing risk in published outputs (ONS, 2021 & 2023). While DP is well-suited to releasing aggregate insights, many downstream uses require repeated access to data for exploration, model development, or sharing with third parties. One technique increasingly used in response is differentially private synthetic data, which aims to preserve population-level properties while offering a more flexible interface than query-based DP mechanisms.

2.1.6 Synthetic Data
Synthetic data is often taxonomized into a standalone category. This is because it produces dataset-shaped artefacts that can be shared and analysed using standard workflows. However, we treat it here as an algorithmic PET because its privacy properties are inherent to the privatising mechanism used to fit and release the generator, the threat model, and any downstream controls. Synthetic data is often the most verbose output of an output-privacy algorithm, rather than a qualitatively different class of protection (Stadler et al., 2022). 

Generative models require significant additional controls and formal constraints to prevent training record memorisation and leakage which could enable membership or attribute inference (Carlini et al., 2019; Hayes et al., 2019). Conversely, when synthetic data is explicitly coupled to a formal privacy definition like DP, it can provide a principled, auditable release mechanism, as illustrated by the NIST DP Synthetic Data Competition and subsequent approaches to DP synthetic data generation (McKenna et al., 2021). To draw an explicit comparison, whilst prior surveys focus on the design of differentially private synthetic data generators (e.g. Jordon et al., 2022), this section treats synthetic data primarily as an output‑privacy interface and evaluates its implications for disclosure risk, governance, and data‑limitation. 

Within statistical disclosure limitation, this approach is typically operationalised by releasing microdata where records are simulated from a model fitted to a dataset, such that released records are indistinguishable from those of real individuals; particularly in settings where synthetic data is intended to preserve fine-grained distributional or individual-level structure rather than acting as a low-utility proxy (Rubin, 1993; Reiter, 2005; Drechsler, 2011). Despite this abstraction, residual disclosure risk can remain and is often underestimated in practice (Rubin, 1993; Abowd & Vilhuber, 2008; Kaabachi et al., 2025). Recent advances in deep generative modelling have amplified interest in this approach. Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) learn a generator to mimic the data distribution via an adversarial discriminator, while VAEs learn a latent-variable encoder-decoder model that supports sampling new records from the learned distribution (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Kingma and Welling, 2014).

Across regulated domains, including healthcare, synthetic data is being utilised to enable analytics and sharing under confidentiality constraints (Assefa et at., 2020). These include open toolkits and large consortia that combine generation with evaluation and governance frameworks (e.g., Patki et al., 2016; SEARCH Consortium, 2024; Ros et al., 2016).  ‘Synthea’, for instance, generates synthetic, realistic patient populations and associated health records for development and research (Synthea, 2024). While Synthea generates data from scratch, other frameworks other frameworks generate synthetic data by fitting data-driven generative models to sensitive source records. The Synthetic Data Vault (SDV), for example, explicitly learns a generative model that captures dependencies to produce statistically and structurally similar records (MIT DAI Group, n.d.). This distincction matters because the dominant privacy risk for data-driven generators is leakage related to training data and release inference, rather than merely removal of identifiers (Qian et al., 2024).  

Moreover, synthetic data’s overall privacy benefits are often overstated or misunderstood. Quantitative evaluations of synthetic data publishing highlight that synthetic datasets often do not protect against inference attacks or do not retain data utility (Stadler et al., 2022), motivating an evaluation-first posture that treats privacy as contingent on measurable evidence rather than as an intrinsic property of synthetic data (e.g., Raisaro et al., 2023; Carmichael et al., 2023). For example, a membership inference attack can often identify whether an outlier record from the original data was likely included in training, because if the generative model reproduces that outlier, or a value that is very close, it signals the individual’s presence (Hayes et al., 2019; Carlini et al., 2019). This has motivated the creation of adversarial privacy auditing toolchains such as TAPAS for stress-testing synthetic releases under explicit threat models (Houssiau et al., 2022). 

Conversely, methods to make generative models differentially private, such as DP-SGD for GANs or VAEs, tend to degrade the fidelity of the generated data significantly (Xie et al., 2018; Torkzadehmahani et al., 2019). Moreover, unlike static noise, distortions in synthetic data are data-dependent and complex, making it hard to predict which statistical properties are preserved or lost in synthetic outputs. While often deemed a useful tool for testing and model development or in conjunction with other formal privacy measures like DP at the point of release, synthetic data is not generally a robust substitute for open public microdata f (Bellovin et al., 2019; Jordan et al., 2022; ODI, 2025). 

Related approaches sometimes described as ‘synthetic’ involve releasing partial, coarsened, or perturbed versions of sensitive data. This includes permute-and-mask methods for releasing location data with reduced precision or adding noise to graphs or networks (Andrés et al., 2013; Sala et al., 2011). These approaches can mitigate certain attacks but generally lack formal guarantees and must be used with caution. In practice, producing a safe, public dataset that remains highly useful is extremely difficult (Stadler et al., 2022). Interactive querying layering PETs such as DP, or intentionally distorted, low-fidelity synthetic data for code and workflow testing,  combined with controlled access to real data in a secure environment, are often more trustworthy strategies (NIST, 2022; Jordan et al., 2024).

2.1.7 Zero-Knowledge Proofs
Zero-knowledge proofs (ZKPs) are cryptographic protocols that allow a prover to convince a verifier that a statement is true without revealing any additional information beyond the statement’s validity (NIST, n.d.; Silde & Takahashi, 2024). Within the framework set out by our proposed logic mode, ZKPs sit within algorithmic PETs because they deliver privacy through their proof objects rather than by virtue of encrypting inputs during processing or by injecting noise at release (NIST, n.d.; Silde & Takahashi, 2024). NIST’s Privacy-Enhancing Cryptography (PEC) materials explicitly frame ZKPs as a core PEC tool and track standardisation efforts and deployments (NIST, n.d.). This placement is consistent with deployment-facing discussions that stress how ZKPs operationalise interfaces that support verifiable privacy claims under explicit security goals (Silde & Takahashi, 2024).

Modern ZKP deployments are strongly shaped by non-interactive proof systems (NIZKs), including zk-SNARK and zk-STARK families, because they enable one-shot proofs that can be verified by many verifiers without interactive rounds (Roelink & El-Hajj, 2024). In blockchain settings, this non-interactive verification property is central to privacy preserving transactions and scalability patterns such as rollups, which is why applied reviews of zk-SNARKs often discuss cryptocurrency and rollup applications and their practical trade-offs (Chen et al., 2023; Roelink & El-Hajj, 2024). Benchmarks and systematic studies show that real-world performance varies across proof systems and implementations, necessitating comparative evaluations of zk-SNARKs, zk-STARKs, and Bulletproof-style constructions in realistic codebases (Roelink & El-Hajj, 2024). A ZKP’s privacy interface is inseparable from engineering choices such as the proving system, toolchain maturity, setup model, and related choices made about transparency and trust (XXX, XXX; XXX, XXX).

ZKPs simultaneously support privacy-proving correctness without revealing sensitive transaction details and verifiability at scale, where verification cost is kept small relative to the computation being attested (Chen et al., 2023). Literature focused on zk-STARK frames ZK systems as a tool to enable public trust and large-scale verifiable computations, allowing for privacy-preserving proofs of non-membership over databases while emphasising transparency and verification scalability (Ben-Sasson et al., 2022). Simultaneously, protocol-level research continues to attempt to refine security claims in widely deployed SNARKs. For instance, a 2024 analysis of PLONK reports a vulnerability in an earlier specification and proves statistical zero-knowledge for a patched version via an explicit simulator (Sefranek, 2024). This emphasises that assurance artefacts must attach to the implemented mechanism, not just the high-level label ‘ZKP’ (XXX, XXX).

Recent work argues that the absence of shared benchmarks and reporting conventions presents a deployment risk (Ernstberger et al., 2023; Roelink & El-Hajj, 2024). zk-Bench attempts to address this reproducibility gap and notes that omissions in evaluation detail can render comparisons effectively non-auditable (Koch, Rotaru & Rechberger, 2025). This highlights that proof size, prover time and verifier time are not stable trade-offs: prover bottlenecks shift with circuit structure and implementation choices, and operational feasibility can hinge on which subroutines dominate a given workload (Roelink & El-Hajj, 2024). For instance, GPU work targeting Halo2 reports that custom-gate evaluation becomes a dominant workload in large zkEVM circuits and motivates end-to-end acceleration because proof generation remains a scaling bottleneck (Tribuste, 2024). Moreover, attempts to mitigate performance bottlenecks can translate into new operational dependencies, such as reliance on parallelism (XXX, XXX). Many deployments are implicitly coupled to specialised hardware or execution protocols to perform at an acceptable latency (halo2, n.d.).

These pressures often push proof generation toward specialised hardware, prover clusters, or third-party infrastructure, shifting sensitive computations and intermediate states outside of the verifier’s trust boundary even when verification remains succinct (Tribuste, 2024). Systems such as Eos mitigate this by delegating proofs across workers, but privacy still depends on explicit non-collusion assumptions and introduces additional system-level complexity that must be engineered and audited (Chiesa et al., 2023). Furthermore, circuit-level misspecification remains a practical failure mode. Analysis of Halo2 circuits targets unused gates and under-constrained cells, highlighting that proof-system security does not ensure correctness of the encoded statement, and that errors in circuit construction or arithemtisation can admit invalid witnesses while proofs still verify (Heidari Soureshjani, 2023).

Beyond blockchains, ZKPs are increasingly presented as a route to data minimisation in identity and credential ecosystems. However, recent work also stresses that end-to-end privacy depends on system features that are often missing or underspecified (Babel & Sedlmeir, 2023; W3C, 2025). Babel & Sedlmeir (2023) argue that existing anonymous credential solutions can lack operational requirements such as scalable revocation and practical integration properties, demonstrating the potential value of general-purpose ZKPs but also highlighting a non-trivial engineering and governance burden. At a standards layer, projects like W3C’s (2025) VC 2.0, show that selective disclosure and related proofs can be a helpful mechanism for limiting over-disclosure. However, current regulatory discussions emphasise that this does not remove the need for careful implementation choices around correlation, replay and verification practices (XXX, XXX). eIDAS 2.0 establishes the European Digital Identity framework, yet recent analysis argues that ZKP integration remains and open implementation question (European Union, 2024). The privacy properties that ZKPs can theoretically enable may not materialise unless the ecosystem converges on concrete technical profiles and assurance processes. 

Finally, it is worth noting that ZKPs are becoming a viable tool in enabling verifiable ML (ZKML). Notwithstanding, there are significant technical constraints: Chen et al. (2024) emphasise that prior ZK-SNARK approaches were limited to unrealistically small models and that compiling realistic models require careful circuit design because equivalent implementations can differ substantially in performance. Their ZKML system uses an optimising compiler to target Halo2 and tests the approach on realistic models while simultaneously demonstrating the importance of circuit layout and gadget choices as direct determinants of feasibility (Chen et al., 2024; XXX, XXX).  More broadly, surveys emphasise that ZK verifiable ML remains fragmented and bottlenecked by proving cost, limited circuit expressiveness, and deployment complexity, so verifiable inference is best treated as workflow specific assurance, not a general substitute for transparency or audit access (Peng et al., 2026).

2.2 Architectural PETs
The encompassed approaches leverage system and software architecture to minimise the exposure of raw data, by keeping sensitive data in controlled, local and isolated processing environments while moving computation or access in and out securely. They are complementary to algorithmic PETs and often used in combination with them.

2.2.1 Federated Learning and Distributed Analytics
FL is a distributed training paradigm in which a central coordinator iteratively trains a shared global model by dispatching the current model to multiple clients that keep their data locally, collecting the clients’ locally computed parameter updates, and aggregating those updates to form the next global model. (McMahan et al., 2017; Kairouz et al., 2021). Only model updates, in the form of weights or gradients, are communicated and aggregated, meaning raw data never leaves the local device, addressing many data-ownership and regulatory concerns.

FL has been successfully demonstrated in scenarios such as cross-device AI, such as Google’s Gboard which predicts text without uploading specific user keystrokes, and cross-organisation collaborations, such as hospitals that have jointly trained medical diagnosis models without sharing patient records (Rieke et al., 2020; Kaissis et al., 2020). Within the context of healthcare deployments, FL can be employed as an operational product rather than a research workflow. NVIDIA’s FLARE framework, for example, can handle elements of federation creation, site approvals, various deployment options, and an administrative provision-start-operate workflow intended to reduce coordination adn configuration friction across sites (NVIDIA, 2021; NVIDIA, n.d.). A similarly deployment-orientated approach is demonstrated by a multicentre NHS study that distributes a preloaded Raspberry Pi, full-stack federated learning appliance to hospital groups to train and evaluate a COVID-19 screening model without centralising patient data; in large part to address the fact that FL adoption at hospitals is often impacted by the need for specialist software and expertise at each site (Soltan et al., 2024).

However, FL alone does not guarantee formal privacy of the model updates. Adversaries can potentially infer information about participants’ data from model updates (Zhu & Han, 2020). Hence, researchers and practitioners often integrate algorithmic PETs into FL. Secure aggregation protocols ensure that the server only sees the sum of updates rather than individual contributions and applying DP to model updates, or final models, limits the information any single data point can leak (Bonawitz et al., 2017; Truex et al., 2019). 

Moreover, despite these enhancements, FL faces several open challenges. Data across participants may have different distributions, which can degrade model performance relative to centrally collected data (Kairouz et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020). Ensuring high accuracy while preserving privacy is especially difficult in scenarios where data is scarce and imbalances (Xu et al., 2021). Moreover, effective FL typically requires multiple rounds of communication, often requiring many participants. Communication costs and straggler effects can be significant creating a practical barrier to scaling federated solutions (Konečný et al., 2016; McMahan et al., 2017). Techniques like model compression, client subsampling, and fewer rounds are used to alleviate this Konečný et al., 2016; Bonawitz et al., 2017). 

In practice, robust federated solutions often require the containing of outlier updates, whether benign due to heterogeneity or adversarial due to poisoning, because naive averaging is fallible under heavy-tailed or Byzantine update distributions (Zhang et al., 2024). Mitigation techniques include robust aggregators, such as median or trimmed-mean-style estimators, and data-driven weighting schemes designed to down-weight anomalous contributions (Li et al., 2022). Complementary approaches attempt to detect malicious clients by exploiting temporal inconsistency in their updates across rounds such that subsequent aggregations are performed on a filtered and more reliable client population (Quan et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022).

Finally, it has been shown that malicious participants can poison updates or manipulate federated training processes if not properly mitigated (Blanchard et al., 2017; Fang et al., 2020). Model inversion or membership inference attacks, determining if a specific individual’s data was in the training set, remain possible if models are overly complex or insufficiently protected (Nasr et al., 2019). Notwithstanding, FL remains a promising architectural PET that is relatively mature in certain applications and can be effectively used in conjunction with cryptographic and DP techniques to enhance privacy (Wei et al., 2020).

2.2.2 Trusted Execution Environments 
TEEs use secure hardware support to create an isolated execution environment, or enclave, on a device or server, so that sensitive code and data can be processed with hardware-enforced secrecy and integrity. The best-known example is Intel’s Software Guard Extensions (SGX), which allows user-level code to execute in an encrypted memory region inaccessible to even the operating system or hypervisor (Costan & Devadas, 2016). By running data analysis inside a TEE, one can achieve a form of hardware-based input privacy (Schuster et al., 2015). Data can be decrypted and processed within the enclave, and only approved outputs are released, while the raw data remains concealed from the rest of the system. 

TEEs have been effectively used to build trusted analytics platforms. In a cross-bank data sharing pilot, multiple banks encrypted their data and sent it to a cloud-based SGX enclave for joint computation of fraud detection models, with the assurance that plaintext data remains inaccessible to the cloud operator and host software (WEF, 2019; BCG, 2021). In an enterprise setting, Microsoft’s Commerce Financial Services payment processing, moving payment workloads to Azure Confidential Computing using Intel SGX to protect sensitive payment instruments and cryptographic key material in use during processing (Intel, 2024). 

In healthcare, TEEs are used as part of a Trusted Research Environments (TREs). However, TREs are often better understood as governed access environments, often structured using the Five Safes, relying on output checking and disclosure control rather than a single output type restriction (GOV.UK, 2025; FiveSafes.org, n.d.; ONS, n.d.; Johnson et al., 2022).  . While TEEs are predominantly employed as implementation mechanisms for ‘safe settings’ within broader controlled access models, TREs are broadly seen as socio-technical control regimes. TEEs are not a substitute for governance controls such as project approvals, researcher accreditation, and output checking (FiveSafes.org, n.d.; ONS, n.d). Surveying similar critiques of TREs, the recent UK Biobank takedown series, documented via repeated GitHub DMCA notices and accompanying commentary, illustrates that privacy assurances hinge on downstream operational controls and continuous monitoring, not deidentified labelling or the perceived governance quality of the source dataset (Rocher & Morley, 2026).

Compared to pure cryptographic approaches, TEEs often offer faster performance for complex computations. Nevertheless, TEEs introduce a degree of residual trust because confidentiality is anchored in vendor-issued attestation and hardware-enforced isolation (ODI, 2024). In turn, the relying party must trust the CPU’s microarchitecture and vendor’s provisioning infrastructure, while attestation itself only proves that a measured enclave is running on a genuine, versioned platform, not that the enclave code is correct. Numerous studies simulating side-channel attacks have demonstrated some of these potential shortcomings, exploiting patterns in memory access, timing, or CPU branch behaviour to infer enclave secrets (Nilsson et al., 2020; Van Bulck et al., 2018). 

While mitigations exist, such as shielding sensitive operations or inserting noise, these attacks show that TEEs are not impregnable and must often be combined with other PETs. Moreover, TEEs have practical constraints. They tend to have limited secure memory and incur performance overhead for context switches and encryption of memory pages, which can bottleneck large-scale analytics (Arnautov et al., 2016; Schuster et al., 2015). Deploying TEEs also often requires specialised hardware and careful management of keys and attestation (Microsoft, 2025). For these reasons, TEEs are currently most useful for scenarios like enabling a neutral third-party compute service or protecting critical segments of larger workflows, like performing a secure aggregation of intermediate results in a federated system. 

TEE-enabled ‘confidential computing’ is now offered as an option by major cloud providers, but research indicates that organisations often lack the expertise required to adopt TEEs securely and to address known vectors, creating a non-trivial learning curve for developers and operators (Geppert et al., 2022; Microsoft, 2025; Amazon Web Services, n.d.; Google, 2026). In academic and industrial contexts, TEEs are often integrated with other PETs. Some hybrid systems use a TEE to coordinate or validate an MPC protocol, or to perform rapid computations and then apply DP noise to the outputs, balancing performance and privacy (Ryffel et al., 2020). In summary, TEEs can reduce reliance on heavy cryptography but shift the trust to hardware and can still be vulnerable to low-level attacks. 

Related confidential computing variants are also emerging for AI-specific workflows, including pilots using GPU-enabled secure enclave technology to enable decentralised model evaluations without exposing underlying data or model artefacts. This was recently demonstrated by an OpenMined pilot, with the UK AI Safety Institute and Anthropic, and in a subsequent NIST CAISI collaboration focused on secure AI evaluations (OpenMined, n.d.; NIST, 2026). 

3. Cross-Cutting Technical Challenges
Researchers and practitioners face recurring engineering and assurance challenges that hinder wider adoption, effective use and trustworthy deployments (Ameur & Bouzefrane, 2023; OECD 2023; Royal Society, 2019). Rather than reviewing PETs purely through domain, or sectoral, specific use cases, we survey commonly found, cross-cutting constraints evidenced in literature and in practical applications, to offer a generalisable assessment of how PET guarantees survive contact with real systems. These include privacy-utility trade-offs, performance limits, integration burdens, and verification artefacts. 

3.1 Privacy-Utility Trade-Off
Throughout this subsection, ‘utility’ refers primarily to analytic fidelity, which can be defined as the extent to which protected outputs support valid inference, accurate modelling, and decision-relevant estimates. This differs from overall workflow usefulness and enablement value, where lower-fidelity artefacts can still be highly valuable for preparatory tasks such as pipeline development, schema validation, feasibility assessments and early-stage experimentation. In these settings, a high-privacy artefact may have limited analytic fidelity yet still increase productivity by allowing work to proceed while access approvals or high-fidelity releases are pending. Our focus here is trade-offs for analytically demanding tasks; we revisit enablement and integration benefits in the implementation and governance discussion.

All PETs must inherently balance privacy protection against the usefulness of data or models (Troncoso et al., 2017). In formal terms, stronger privacy guarantees often imply reduced analytic fidelity, in contexts where privacy is achieved by introducing noise or distortion. This is most clearly demonstrated by DP, where smaller ε yields more noise and lower accuracy. By contrast, data-in-use cryptographic PETs, such as SMPC and FHE, can preserve the functional correctness of the computation over protected inputs, but typically shift the trade-off onto system costs such as compute, latency, and communication (OECD, 2023; Cheon et al., 2022). Many cryptographic PETs preserve exact functional correctness over protected inputs, but they typically incur substantial protocol overhead (Li et al., 2020). This is particularly apparent in use cases that require interactive communication and high bandwidth, where end-to-end cost is often dominated by communication and protocol interaction rather than local compute, making feasibility strongly workload and network-dependent (Evans et al., 2018; Merino & Cabero-Holgueras, 2023). Examples include privacy-preserving model evaluation and training via secure computation frameworks (Knott et al., 2021 & 2022), and cross-institutional fraud detection analytics (Georgieva Belorgey & Carpov, 2024; Mastercard, 2024). 

In settings where privacy is achieved by constraining or transforming what is shared, the trade-off manifests more directly as degraded accuracy or convergence. In FL, statistical heterogeneity, or non-independent and identically distributed (IID) client data, can reduce accuracy and hinder convergence relative to centralised learning baselines, with empirical studies reporting substantial degradation under highly skewed partitions (Zhao et al., 2018; Seo et al., 2025). For synthetic data, large-scale evaluations show that generators frequently fail to simultaneously prevent inference attacks and preserve downstream utility, and that the privacy-utility trade-off is hard to predict ex ante (Stadler et al., 2022). Perhaps less obviously, operational robustness measures that filter or down-weight anomalous client updates can improve security against poisoning but can also interact with non-IID heterogeneity and therefore shift the privacy-utility frontier in ways that require empirical tuning and monitoring (Zhang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2022). Taken together, the much of the literature and practical real-world applications underline that there is no universal, ‘best’ PET. Different mechanisms shift utility loss onto different resources, such as accuracy, latency, communication or modelling flexibility, so practical choices are constrained by the specific workload, threat model, and tolerance for degradation. 

Understanding and meaningfully quantifying trade-offs, through techniques like privacy budget tuning, privacy loss metrics and empirical attack-based simulations, is a significant, ongoing research focus. This is apparent in work which evaluates the gap between formal DP guarantees and measured leakage under inference attacks in learning settings, where improving utility via weaker noise can materially increase observed membership leakage (Jayaraman & Evans, 2019). However, meaningfully evidencing trade-offs in a generalisable manner across deployments is often difficult. Threat assessments depend on contextual factors, such as the attacker’s capabilities, available auxiliary information, interaction patterns, and the release interface, yet governance and risk owners typically need conclusions that transfer across operational settings. In practice, institutional adoption is further constrained by legal and regulatory uncertainty. Decisionmakers often lack authoritative clarity on what good looks like, and organisations may be unwilling to incur the cost of PET implementation alongside residual compliance and reputational risk. This can create a coordination problem in which suppressed adoption limits the feedback loops and reference deployments needed to standardise practice. On a market or practitioner level this is occasionally framed as an incentive failure. NIST’s initial public draft IR 8588 ‘A Community-Driven Differential Privacy Deployment Registry’ (2025) bridges a possible response to this challenge in that it proposes a structured, moderated repository of DP deployments intended to support shared learning, establish norms, and provide snapshots of practice that can inform both engineers and regulators. 

Most real-world problems require a multi-layered approach combining multiple PETs and more traditional information governance techniques to get closer to ideal privacy-utility frontiers and careful case-by-case evaluations are essential (Le Ny & Pappas, 2015; OECD 2023). Ultimately, achieving measurable privacy while preserving utility for complex analytics remains an open problem in many scenarios, warranting continued research (Dwork et al., 2021). Finally, it is worth caveating that PET use does not necessarily change the legal status of the data artefact, meaning baseline compliance duties remain materially unchanged even where technical risk is reduced. For instance, outputs may remain personal data: an issue repeatedly raised in guidance on identifiability and in PET adoption discussions (ICO, 2021, 2023; EDPB, 2021). 

3.2 Performance and Scalability Constraints
Many PETs impose significant computational or communication overhead, though recent studies highlight that the scale and location of these costs are increasingly workload, protocol, and hardware-dependent rather than well captured by a single multiplier. Previous evaluations of SMPC cite computation slowdowns of factors of 10-1000× and FHE slowdowns in the rough range of 10³-10^5× (Evans et al., 2018; Halevi & Shoup, 2020). These figures are relative to unprotected computations for many workloads and should be read as indicative orders of magnitude rather than stable constants (Evans et al., 2018; Halevi & Shoup, 2020). This can make big-data analysis or real-time processing infeasible and can often mean that cost scales worse-than-linearly with the number of released statistics and the size or depth of the protected computation (Dwork & Roth, 2014; Abadi et al., 2016). While application-specific protocols such as Interoperable Private Attribution (IPA), by Meta and Mozilla, demonstrate the feasibility of privacy-preserving attribution in real-time bidding systems, their performance remains highly context-dependent and cannot be generalised as evidence for scalability (Meta & Mozilla, 2023).

More recent benchmarking demonstrates the persistence and variability of these costs. A 2025 cross-library HE benchmark reports per-operation timings ranging from 0.021ms to 0.059ms across HElib, SEAL, OpenFHE and Lattigo under different schemes, with SEAL showing memory use of appropriately 15MB, while FHE analyses of AI-style workloads emphasise that cost varies sharply with bit strength, SIMD parallelisation, and the mix of linear and non-linear operations (Pambudi et al., 2025; Xue et al., 2025). Practical scaling is therefore usually not purely determined by a PET’s overall speed, but by which resource becomes the bottleneck in a deployment setting, be it computation, communication, memory, or parameter regime, and how sensitive that bottleneck is to implementation choices.

For implementations leveraging DP, high-dimensional analyses typically require answering large workloads, such as many marginal counts or iterative training steps, so privacy loss composes across queries and iterations while the noise required for a fixed overall privacy budget increases accordingly (Dwork and Roth, 2014; Abadi et al., 2016). In cryptographic PETs, runtime and communication scale with the size of the underlying arithmetic circuit, which grows with the number of records and features in typical analytics pipelines (Riazi et al., 2018; Dathathri et al., 2020). In practice, many secure computation systems initially constraint functionality to restricted aggregate outputs rather than arbitrary model training, because broader analytics can be expensive under strong privacy guarantees (Archer et al., 2018; Schneider, 2020). 
Existing approaches to improve performance therefore tend to concentrate on: 
I.	Protocol specialisation and mixed-protocol design
II.	System-level acceleration
The ‘Chameleon’ framework, for example, reduces online overhead by executing linear operations with additively shared values while outsources nonlinearities to garbled circuits, with most heavy cryptographic workloads pushed to an offline phase. The framework aims to tailor protocol mixes to the computation structure to improve end-to-end cost (Riazi et al., 2018). 
At a systems level, recent work has attempted to speed up hardware implementations and optimisations. ‘CrypTen’, a privacy-preserving ML framework that supports SMPC, reports GPU support and enables efficient, private evaluations of ML models (Knott et al., 2021). CryptoNets, which leverages Microsoft SEAL HE, demonstrates that neural network inference can be executed over encrypted inputs with high throughput, while keeping both inputs and predictions encrypted from the server, and algorithmic improvements in HE libraries have substantially accelerated core linear-algebraic subroutines (Gilad-Bachrach et al., 2016; Halevi and Shoup, 2018). 
Despite advances, scaling PETs to large data and model regimes remains challenging without substantial compute resources or problem reformulations, because overheads accumulate across realistic workflows and often shift rather than disappear (Gilad-Bachrach et al., 2016; Knott et al., 2021; Xue et al., 2025). Simultaneously, recent private-training results show that the feasible frontier is moving. Google’s VaultGemma is presented as a 1B-parameter open model trained from scratch with DP; though the accompanying discussion emphasises that DP training still requires markedly larger batch sizes and higher compute to manage the privacy-utility trade-off (Sinha & McKenna, 2025). Similarly, performance constraints also interact directly with privacy parameters in iterative methods. For example, DP-SGD explicitly requires privacy accounting across training steps: the overall privacy and utility profile depends on how noise and sampling are budgeted over the number of iterations, so engineering decisions that impact iteration count, batch structure, or noise calibration can shift both accuracy and the achieved privacy guarantee (Abadi et al., 2016).

It is important to recognise that end-to-end system costs such as cryptographic preprocessing, communication, memory pressure and runtime integration often determine feasibility. Higher-level toolchains that reduce prerequisite cryptographic expertise and compilers that automate scheme-specific constraints is central to wider adoption (Dathathri et al., 2020). Industry reporting underscores that specialised hardware is increasingly being explored to make FHE practical at scale, such as Intel’s ‘Heracles’ prototype which works by accelerating FHE workloads by orders of magnitude, reinforcing the expectation that usability and performance improvements will be driven as much by systems engineering as by new cryptographic primitives (Moore, 2026). Alongside these digital accelerators, developments in photonic computing are demonstrating the potential to reduce the computational burden of encrypted workloads, though this remains an early commercial direction rather than a mature deployment (Moore, 2026; Ali et al., 2025).  Prominent examples include Optalysys and Google’s HEIR trial, integrating photonic processing into an FHE compiler toolchain (Optalysys, 2024). 

Finally, since performance is highly sensitive to deployment parameters, comparable evaluation requires reproducible benchmarking that discloses the relevant system parameter regime, and hardware assumptions, rather than reporting isolated speedups. Relevant deployment parameters include party count and adversary model employed for MPC, scheme and parameter sets in HE, and enclave memory and transitions costs for TEEs. Recent work across HE, TEEs, MPC, and ZKP benchmarking highlight that deployability claims are inseparable from the specific library or compiler used, the chosen parameters, and the execution environment, making benchmark artefacts and reporting conventions part of the assurance evidence base rather than a supplementary detail (Dathathri et al., 2020; Pambudi et al., 2025; Lutsch et al., 2025; Koch, Rotaru & Rechberger, 2025).

3.3 Integration, Usability and Operational Risk
PETs remain difficult to operationalise at scale because problems lie as much in integration as in cryptography. Deployments must account for safe parameterisation, implementation assurance, workflow fit, interoperability with governance, and organisational capacity, or theoretical guarantees collapse in practice. End-to-end implementation risk is increasingly documented: a recent SoK analyses vulnerabilities in SNARK implementations across circuit, frontend, backend, and integration layers, undermining the assumption that protocol proofs automatically translate into system security (Chaliasos et al., 2024). Regulatory-facing commentary likewise stresses that PETs exist on a spectrum and that privacy representations must match the actual implementation and threat model, reinforcing the need for explicit artefacts and audible claims (FTC, 2024). 

Similarly, differentially private systems require decisions around clipping norms, sampling probabilities and composition strategies, while HE and SMPC stacks expose scheme parameters, precision and packing plans, corruption thresholds and pre-processing sizes; misspecification of the parameterisation phase can silently void guarantees or destroy data utility (Dwork and Roth, 2014; Abadi et al., 2016; Mironov, 2017). An emerging mitigation technique involves adopting vetted accountants and libraries with mechanised reasoning, and to require explicit disclosure of privacy budgets and composition assumptions in model or dataset artefacts (NIST, 2023; Barthe et al., 2021; Dwork, Kohli and Mulligan, 2019; Mitchell et al., 2021). For HE and MPC, higher-level frameworks that surface cost and precision trade-offs reduce configuration risk and improve reproducibility (Knott et al., 2021; Dathathri et al., 2020).

Assurance hinges on tangibly demonstrating that an implemented pipeline provides a reliable degree of privacy, or claims, rather than relying on abstract protocols. For DP this involves end-to-end accounting with numerically tight bounds, sanity checks on sensitivity and query semantics, and, where feasible, proof carrying components (Barthe et al., 2021; NIST, 2023). For HE and SMPC, as well as TEE-accelerated designs, the focus expands to correctness of low-level kernels, constant time implementations and resistance to microarchitectural leakage (Kocher, 1996; Yarom & Falkner, 2014). Enclave-based deployments must also treat attestation, provisioning and the vendor trust anchor as first-class risks and combine TEEs with algorithmic PETs, such as secure aggregation and DP at release, to maintain defence-in-depth (Costan and Devadas, 2016; Schuster et al., 2015; Nilsson, Nikbakht Bideh and Brorsson, 2020; Bonawitz et al., 2017). Interface design remains a bottleneck. Although projects such as OpenDP, or SmartNoise, and PySyft implement PETs as composable building blocks, integrating them into analytical workflows requires declarative specifications of protected computations to prevent accidental nonprivate fallbacks, automatic transformation passes, and reproducible artefacts, such as fixed seeds, parameter manifests, and version-locked accountants, so that claims are auditable (Gaboardi et al., 2020; Ryffel et al., 2018; Dathathri et al., 2020; NIST, 2023).
Interoperability and governance often shape technical choices, requiring information regarding composition regime, sensitivity assumptions, protocol selections, attestation outputs and evaluation metrics, alongside the model or dataset to satisfy internal review and external audit (Royal Society, 2019; OECD, 2023; ICO, 2023; Dwork, Kohli and Mulligan, 2019). Cloud confidential computing offerings, such as Azure Confidential Computing, AWS Nitro Enclaves, Google Confidential VMs, lower entry costs for hardware isolation and workload attestation, but empirical studies emphasise that enclave memory limits, attestation policy engineering and operational key management remain material adoption barriers, and that hybrid PETs are typically required to close residual risks (Microsoft, 2025; Amazon Web Services, n.d.; Google, 2026; Geppert et al., 2022).
Finally, integration success correlates not only with organisational capacity, but also with the ability to secure stakeholder alignment, define roles clearly, and obtain sustained implementation support. Recent work by the Open Data Institute (ODI, 2024) frames successful deployment in terms of stakeholder engagement, role allocation, iterative design, and securing funding for later phases, arguing that adoption barriers are not purely technical even where a mechanism is mature (OECD, 2023). A related hurdle is the scarcity of cross-disciplinary expertise. Effective PET deployments often require teams that can jointly reason about threat models, cryptographic assumptions, privacy accounting, software engineering, governance constraints, and utility target, yet such combinations of expertise remain difficult to assemble in practice. (Cranor & Sadeh, 2013; OECD, 2023; Spiekermann & Cranor, 2008). 

Effective programmes therefore pair PET adoption with training, explicit risk registers, and process maturity around parameter change control, artefact logging and incident response (Royal Society, 2019; ICO, 2023; OECD, 2023). PETs become dependable only when parameterisation, implementation, evidence and operations are engineered as rigorously as the underlying proofs (Gaboardi et al., 2020; Ryffel et al., 2018; Dathathri et al., 2020). The combination of standards and guidance with higher-level compilers and libraries is narrowing the gap, but production grade usability still hinges on defence-in-depth designs and reproducible, auditable artefacts (Halevi and Shoup, 2018; Moore, 2026).

3.4 Composability and Interface Hazards
Deployments rarely rely on a single PET mechanism; instead, privacy guarantees arise from how mechanisms compose across interfaces (data ingress, computation boundary, model update exchange, output release) and how trust shifts across actors and platforms (Royal Society, 2023; OECD, 2023). Consequently, composability is best treated as an engineering problem of aligning (i) leakage channels and (ii) assurance artefacts across layers, rather than as a purely theoretical property of any one primitive (Near et al., 2025; FTC, 2024).
For DP, composition is explicit and cumulative: repeated releases and iterative learning require privacy accounting across heterogeneous mechanisms, and practical guidance frames “privacy hazards” as systematic pitfalls when mathematical DP is realised in software (Near et al., 2025). This motivates artefacts such as accountants, parameter manifests, and transparent disclosure of ε choices, reflecting the argument that DP can be “privacy in name” without documented implementation decisions and shared learning (Dwork et al., 2019; OpenDP Registry, 2026; Nanayakkara et al., 2025).

For federated learning, composability hazards arise because “data stays local” does not imply privacy of updates: model updates can leak information, motivating layered designs that combine FL with secure aggregation and DP (Bonawitz et al., 2016/2017; Wei et al., 2019). Recent protocol work further shows that introducing stronger DP techniques (e.g., correlated noise across rounds) can be incompatible with baseline secure aggregation assumptions unless the aggregation layer itself evolves, motivating new cryptographic primitives such as secure stateful aggregation for DP FTRL under an untrusted server model (Ball et al., 2024). Here, composability is a concrete protocol system co design question: privacy and utility depend on how aggregation, dropout resilience, and accounting interact across rounds (Ball et al., 2024; Bonawitz et al., 2016/2017).

For TEEs, composability concerns centre on trust shifts: enclaves can reduce cryptographic overhead but introduce reliance on attestation and vendor trust anchors, and organisational analyses emphasise that secure deployment depends on operational maturity rather than enclave isolation alone (Geppert et al., 2022). In governed analytics, TREs operationalise composability through “safe settings” plus “safe outputs,” where outputs are screened and cleared via workflow rather than assumed safe by output type, making output checking decisions and logs a key assurance artefact (ONS, 2024; ADR UK, 2024).

For ZKPs, composition hazards appear both in performance dependencies and in correctness boundaries: proofs can verify while the encoded statement is wrong if circuits are under constrained, and empirical work shows vulnerabilities across the SNARK stack, so assurance must include circuit analysis, toolchain scrutiny, and integration testing rather than relying solely on cryptographic proofs (Chaliasos et al., 2024; Heidari Soureshjani et al., 2023). Performance composability also matters proving feasibility depends on the full stack, and benchmarking frameworks explicitly motivate reproducible measurement because results are otherwise reported “in isolation,” limiting comparability and auditability (Ernstberger et al., 2023). Hardware level studies show that bottlenecks can concentrate in specific kernels such as NTT on GPUs and in custom gate evaluation for Halo2 based circuits, implying that deployment risk includes hardware and implementation coupling, not just proof system choice (Verma et al., 2025; Tribuste, 2024; zcash/halo2, n.d.). This has produced benchmarking artefacts and infrastructure efforts across layers, frameworks, harnesses, and hardware primitive databases, reflecting a move toward comparable, reproducible evaluation as part of assurance (Ernstberger et al., 2023; Ingonyama, 2023; zk Harness, n.d.).

Finally, for synthetic data and de identification, composability hazards are strongly lifecycle dependent: residual re identification risk depends on context, downstream recipients, and auxiliary information, motivating frameworks that treat privacy as ongoing risk management rather than a one time transformation (ISO/IEC, 2022; IAPP, 2023). Across these PET families, the practical implication is that deployments should specify a composability narrative (“what is protected, where, under which trust assumptions”) and bundle corresponding artefacts, privacy budgets and accountants, attestation evidence, benchmark reports, circuit checks, and output clearance logs: to make privacy claims inspectable and maintainable over time (Royal Society, 2023; Near et al., 2025; ONS, 2024).

3.5 Verification, Evidence, and Standardisation
Verification for PETs must move beyond protocol level proofs towards evidence that is comparable across systems and composable across layers. For DP, this centres on reportable privacy loss profiles with vetted accountants, reproducible seeds, and explicit disclosure of composition assumptions and sensitivity bounds (Abadi et al., 2016; Barthe et al., 2021; NIST, 2023). For cryptographic PETs and TEEs, verification must include side channel posture, constant-time constraints, and attestation artefacts that are auditable alongside model or query outputs (Costan & Devadas, 2016; Schuster et al., 2015; Nilsson et al., 2020). Critically, cross-PET composability requires reasoning about interfaces. When DP is used in combination with FL or when MPC is orchestrated inside TEEs, privacy can degrade unless the trust and leakage assumptions align and are tracked over time (Dwork & Roth, 2014; Mironov, 2017; Busom et al., 2019).

Privacy degradation in composed PET systems arises when leakage channels not covered by one mechanism’s threat model, such as gradient-level reconstruction attacks and participation inference in FL, interact with another mechanism’s guarantees, such as naively applied record-level DP. Empirical demonstrations have shown that this can lead to failures like property and membership inference despite formal DP claims, or side-channel-mediated leakage when MPC protocols executed inside TEEs violate assumed hardware isolation (Hitaj et al., 2017; Nasr et al., 2019; Carlini et al., 2021; Van Bulck et al., 2018).

Standardisation is progressing but remains fragmented. NIST offers testable guidance for DP evaluation and reporting, while ISO and IEC standards codify terminology and deidentification frameworks that can aid procurement and audit (NIST, 2023; ISO/IEC 20889, 2018; ISO/IEC 27559, 2022). However, there is not yet a universally accepted, autorotative, and reliably consistent auditing regime for PETs and privacy guarantees. Current practice relies on a mix of independent or organisation-specific audits, open toolchains and minimal assurance bundles, such as privacy budgets, composition manifests, and attestation evidence, to support transparency and accountability (Royal Society, 2019; OECD, 2023; ICO, 2023). 

Recent ICO research highlights that PET adoption faces several barriers, including lack of organisation understanding which hampers internal buy-in, and a lack of regulatory clarity, particularly on assessing the status of data when using PETs (ICO, 2023). Setting appropriate levels of DP parameters remains context-specific and subject to ongoing academic research. Regulators emphasise that values must be assessed case-by-case, considering dataset specificity, available auxiliary information, and the means reasonably likely to be used for identification (ICO, 2023; NIST, 2023). Practitioner-facing syntheses highlight the importance of deployment transparency, parameter disclosure, and the need for formalism to avoid ad hoc privacy failures. Real-world DP deployments increasingly publish ε/δ values and privacy units as auditable artefacts, while technical analyses highlight implementation pitfalls such as vulnerabilities on finite-precision computers, reinforcing that guarantees can collapse in practice if not carefully engineered (Desfontaines, 2026; PEPRe/USENIX, 2026).

Within the context of TREs, ‘safe outputs’ are operationalised as elements of controlled workflows, such as output requests, screening for disclosure risk and scope permissions, embedding output-checking logs and clearance decisions as an assurance artefact rather than an abstract principle (ONS, 2024; ADR UK, 2024).

4. Comparative Nuances in PET Adoption...
Rather than treating PET adoption as a sequence of isolated sectoral case studies, this section applies the implementation-centred lens developed above to show how families of mechanisms are selected, combined, and constrained differently across operational settings. The comparative focus is therefore not simply on where PETs appear, but on how sectoral requirements shape viable deployment patterns: which trust assumptions are acceptable, which interfaces are operationally realistic, which assurance artefacts are needed to support internal approval or external scrutiny, and which bottlenecks prevent techniques from moving beyond pilots. 

To keep this discussion compact and resilient to rapid changes in tools and vendors, the section compares PETs primarily at the level of algorithmic and architectural categories and then maps the recurrent combinations of those categories to sectoral usage, assurance posture, and maturity. In this way, sectoral variation is treated as evidence for the broader argument of the paper: PET viability depends less on abstract technical capability than on whether privacy claims remain coherent, inspectable, and workable under real deployment constraints. The most mature and widely adopted deployments consistently pair technical PET stacks with robust governance mechanisms: procedural review, output checking, audit trails, and institutional accountability. Across sectors, adoption is not driven by privacy sensitivity alone, but by whether a PET or combination of techniques fits the sector’s dominant assurance regime. Public-sector settings often favour governed environments and inspectable output control; healthcare requires privacy claims that do not undermine clinical or scientific validity; finance rewards technically strong approaches when they unlock otherwise impossible cross-party analytics; and platform settings favour mechanisms that can be integrated into large-scale production systems with disciplined accounting and monitoring.

While PET adoption cannot be meaningfully understood, compared, or advanced by cataloguing techniques or sectoral use cases alone, sectoral mapping provides valuable context for how PETs are deployed and evaluated in practice. The following subsections illustrate how PET adoption is shaped by sector-specific assurance regimes, operational constraints, and analytical purposes, rather than by privacy sensitivity alone. By examining and summarising representative literature and examples from healthcare, official statistics, finance, and technology platforms, we highlight how PET combinations, parameterisation, and evidence artefacts interact with sectoral requirements and risk trade-offs. This approach reinforces the logic model and comparative artefacts introduced above, showing that the viability of a PET depends not only on its technical properties but also on its role within a workflow, the trust assumptions it introduces, and the form of evidence it produces. 

These sectoral mappings are not intended as exhaustive catalogues, but as illustrative cases that support structured reasoning about cross-cutting limitations, sector-purpose alignment, and adoption hurdles. Section 5 builds directly on this foundation, abstracting these observations into a prospective framework for assessing PET combinations across contexts and supporting systematic, evidence-driven decision-making.

4.1 Healthcare: Sectoral Mapping
Healthcare represents both one of the most sensitive categories of personal information and one of the most valuable resources for improving population health. This duality has driven sustained interest in PET solutions that enable innovation while maintaining privacy (Goldacre, 2022). Healthcare research often relies on TEEs and established encryption standards for operational services. TREs, also known as secure data environments (SDEs), are widely adopted and often employ the Five Safes framework established by the ONS in the 2010s. The NHS England Secure Data Environment and ten regional SDEs form a national network for secure healthcare data access, with the Goldacre Review (2022) catalysing the SDE approach and subsequent government policy.

Federated analytics platforms such as OpenSAFELY, developed by the Bennett Institute for Applied Data Science at the University of Oxford for NHS England, exemplify real-world deployment of distributed analytics in UK healthcare. OpenSAFELY analyses large, sensitive datasets through an abstraction layer. Data never leaves source storage, and researchers only see aggregate results from vetted queries. This approach has enabled over ninety published research studies, demonstrating scalable privacy-preserving analytics (OpenSAFELY, n.d.). Healthcare organisations routinely also apply methods to anonymise and mask data, keeping individual privacy paramount while supporting research and operational needs. Moreover, DP is widely investigated for population-level analytics. Mohammadi (2026) identified seventy-four studies achieving acceptable performance under moderate privacy budgets, but most struggled under stricter budgets. The review highlights fairness as an under-researched area, especially relevant given systematic biases in healthcare. 

Beyond live services, healthcare research increasingly explores algorithmic PETs. The Federated Learning Imaging Platform (FLIP), led by King’s College London’s UK AI Centre for Value Based Healthcare, utilises NVIDIA Flare across five NHS trusts for AI studies in stroke diagnosis and prostate cancer detection (Roth et al., 2025). SMPC is of particular interest for linking data across primary, secondary, and community care, and for distributed genomics and clinical trials (Labao & Adorna, 2022). HE has been demonstrated for survival analysis on biomedical research data using federated analytics based on multiparty homomorphic encryption (Froelicher et al., 2021). The US-UK PETs Prize challenge (2022–2023) included a healthcare track, demonstrating the need for combining multiple PETs to enable collaborative pandemic surveillance analysis. The challenge highlighted the difficulty of achieving robust model performance in private settings, especially with imbalanced case data. Metrics for healthcare PET benchmarking rely on domain-specific and clinically acceptable measures, such as AUROC and task performance, comparing private and non-private baselines. Epsilon values between one and ten are commonly considered, and membership inference attack rates are monitored as an extra defence due to the sensitivity of healthcare data.

4.2 Public Sector and Official Statistics: Sectoral Mapping
The public sector, particularly in the realm of official statistics, has long been at the forefront of privacy-preserving data analysis. The sensitivity of census, demographic, and administrative data, combined with strong accountability and transparency requirements, has driven the adoption of PETs that balance utility, privacy, and public trust (ONS, 2023; Royal Society, 2019). TREs and secure data environments SDEs are now often standard for accessing and analysing official statistics in the UK and internationally. These environments typically implement the Five Safes framework, which governs safe projects, people, data, settings, and outputs, ensuring that privacy risks are managed throughout the data lifecycle (ONS, 2023). Output checking and audit trails are integral, with research outputs subject to disclosure control before release.

DP has emerged as a key mechanism for protecting aggregate statistics. The ONS and the US Census Bureau have both deployed DP in recent census releases, injecting calibrated noise to reduce re-identification risk while maintaining statistical validity (U.S. Census Bureau, 2021; ONS, 2023). However, these deployments have highlighted the trade-offs between privacy and accuracy, with independent evaluations noting challenges in balancing respondent privacy against data quality, especially for small-area statistics and minority populations (Kenny et al., 2021; Muralidhar et al., 2025). Sectoral adoption is shaped by the need for solutions that are not only technically robust but also auditable and legally defensible. Regulatory guidance from bodies such as the ICO and international standards such as ISO/IEC 20889, 2018 and ISO/IEC 27559, 2022, reinforce the importance of explicit privacy accounting, transparent parameter choices, and context-specific risk assessment. The ONS Secure Research Service operationalises these principles through controlled workflows, output screening, and detailed clearance logs (ONS, 2024).

Recent initiatives, such as the European Commission’s Digital Markets Act (DMA) specification for Google Search data sharing, exemplify the move towards ‘assurance-by-design’, where anonymisation requirements are coupled with governance, audit, and eligibility criteria (European Commission, 2026; Dig.watch, 2026). Metrics for benchmarking PETs in official statistics include privacy loss parameters, such as ε in DP, disclosure risk measures, and utility comparisons to non-private baselines. The sector’s experience demonstrates that successful PET adoption depends on integrating technical mechanisms with governance, legal frameworks, and ongoing evaluation.

4.3 Finance: Sectoral Mapping
The finance sector is characterised by strong commercial incentives for joint analysis across organisational boundaries, often without pooling raw data. PETs are increasingly deployed to enable collaborative analytics, fraud detection, and regulatory compliance while maintaining confidentiality and trust (Mastercard, 2024; Archer et al., 2018). SMPC is a prominent PET in finance, allowing multiple parties, such as banks or insurers, to jointly compute functions over their private inputs without revealing those inputs. Real-world deployments include secure collaborative credit scoring, cross-bank fraud detection, and privacy-preserving benchmarking, where SMPC protocols are tailored to the adversary model and performance requirements (Archer et al., 2018; Froelicher et al., 2021). 

HE is also gaining traction, enabling computation on encrypted data for tasks such as encrypted analytics, risk modelling, and regulatory reporting. Industry-led pilots, such as IBM’s HE4Cloud, demonstrate the feasibility of privacy-preserving machine learning and analytics in financial services, although performance bottlenecks remain a challenge for large-scale adoption (IBM, 2025; Halevi & Shoup, 2020). ZKPs are increasingly used for transaction verification and compliance, allowing parties to prove correctness or eligibility without revealing underlying data. ZKPs support privacy-preserving audits, regulatory reporting, and verifiable credentials, with applications in blockchain-based finance and cross-border payments (Chen et al., 2023; Roelink & El-Hajj, 2024). In a similar fashion to other sectors, DP is explored for population-level analytics, such as customer segmentation and market research, but its adoption is limited by the trade-off between privacy and utility, especially in settings where accuracy is important (Jayaraman & Evans, 2019).

Sectoral adoption is shaped by the need for solutions that are technically robust, auditable, and compatible with regulatory frameworks. Regulatory guidance emphasises threat-model clarity, implementation audit, and benchmarked performance as key assurance artefacts (ICO, 2023; OECD, 2023). Metrics for benchmarking PETs in finance include privacy loss parameters, computational overhead, and utility comparisons to non-private baselines.
The finance sector’s experience demonstrates that successful PET adoption depends on integrating technical mechanisms with governance, legal frameworks, and ongoing evaluation, enabling otherwise impossible cross-party analytics while maintaining confidentiality and compliance.

4.4 Technology: Sectoral Mapping
Technology platforms and consumer AI services operate at massive scale, generating continuous streams of telemetry, behavioural data, and model updates. The need to balance privacy, utility, and operational efficiency has driven the adoption of PETs that can be integrated directly into production pipelines and infrastructure (Google, 2024; Royal Society, 2023).

DP is widely deployed for telemetry collection and population-level analytics. Major platforms such as Google and Apple have implemented local DP mechanisms for user telemetry, advertising measurement, and usage statistics, injecting noise at the device level to protect individual data while enabling aggregate insights (Erlingsson et al., 2014; Tang et al., 2017; Google, 2024). Google’s Privacy Sandbox and Apple’s DP telemetry are prominent examples, with real-world deployments emphasising the importance of parameter governance, continuous accounting, and transparent disclosure of privacy budgets (Ghazi et al., 2025). FL is increasingly used for distributed model training, allowing platforms to improve AI services without centralising raw user data. Google’s Gboard keyboard prediction and Apple’s on-device learning are notable deployments, where only model updates are aggregated, and privacy is further enhanced through secure aggregation protocols and differential privacy applied to model updates (Hard et al., 2018; Bonawitz et al., 2017; Kairouz et al., 2021).

Trusted execution environments (TEEs) and secure aggregation are used to protect data in use, enabling confidential computing for AI workloads and decentralised analytics. Cloud providers such as Microsoft Azure, AWS, and Google Cloud offer confidential computing options, supporting attested execution and hardware isolation for sensitive workloads (Microsoft, 2025; Amazon Web Services, n.d.; Google, 2026).

Sectoral adoption is shaped by the need for scalable, auditable, and operationally efficient PETs that can be integrated into platform pipelines. Metrics for benchmarking include privacy loss parameters, utility measures, and system performance, with continuous monitoring and interface discipline essential for maintaining privacy guarantees at scale. The experience of technology platforms demonstrates that successful PET adoption depends on integrating technical mechanisms with robust parameter governance, transparent accounting, and seamless infrastructure integration, enabling privacy-preserving analytics and AI services for millions of users.

5. Cross-Cutting Limitations and Potential Future Directions
Several persistent limitations and open questions cut across PET typologies, combinations, and sectoral deployments. Rather than listing use cases or tools, this section outlines a framework for understanding these limitations as a guide for future research and implementation, and assurance, including the role of standards, evaluative conventions and legal-regulatory interpretation in determining whether privacy claims are credible, comparable, and actionable in practice. A key challenge is the assurance gap. PETs can fail if their artefacts, such as privacy budgets in DP or attestation in TEEs, are not interpretable or comparable across domains. Composability hazards are also significant. Combining multiple PETs often introduces new risks, such as interface mismatches, untracked leakage, or fragmented assurance, that are not visible when mechanisms are evaluated in isolation. The utility and acceptability of PETs are highly context-dependent, and mapping sector to purpose is essential for understanding both the strengths and the limits of PET adoption. Building out this mapping with concrete, real-world examples, especially where PETs have enabled analyses that were previously infeasible, remains an important area for future work.

Thresholds for actionability are another cross-cutting concern. Technical outputs, such as confidence intervals, standard errors, or suppression rates, must be assessed for their practical and clinical significance, not just their statistical properties. The point at which PET-enabled results become actionable evidence is often a matter of expert judgement, not a fixed metric. Similarly, no technical solution can substitute for robust governance. The most successful PET deployments are those that integrate technical artefacts with institutional processes for review, audit, and continuous improvement. To address these limitations, future work should focus on developing frameworks that link PET combinations to sector-purpose pairs with explicit mapping of assurance artefacts, governance requirements, and the legal or regulatory assumptions under which privacy claims are interpreted or realised. 

Capturing and comparing cross-cutting limitations, such as composability, assurance, and usability, in a structured, evidence-seeking way that goes beyond the likes of compliance checklists is a potential means of bridging or communicating prospective limitations. Designing standardised, machine-readable artefacts that explicitly encode privacy assumptions and limitations, alongside evidence on which claims rest, and integrating them into an engineering and deployment workflow might offer a transparent interface between technical implementation and higher-level review. Such an approach might document how privacy is achieved in a specific system configuration, detailing what mechanisms are used, how they are parameterised, what threat models are assumed, and what residual risks remain, without requiring full disclosure of underlying data or code that would pose a security or privacy risk. Decoupling privacy assurances from proprietary tooling may also help rebalance the PET ecosystem, reducing reliance on opaque, vendor-specific implementations an enabling more equitable participation by open-source projects, smaller organisations and public-sector actors through shared interoperable representations of privacy guarantees. 

This potential approach shifts focus from abstract assurances, that might promise the use of DP or that data never leaves a secure enclave, towards operationally meaningful claims that can be compared, inspected and reasoned about across varying contexts. Such artefacts could also support different degrees of disclosure depending on the setting. For instance, enabling public transparency and external audits in research deployments, while remaining restricted, partial, or internal-only in sensitive environments such as national security or law-enforcement systems. The underpinning aim is context-appropriate legibility, making privacy guarantees explicit to the parties who must rely on them. 

Encoding this information in a structured, interoperable manner makes such dependencies explicit and reusable. In practice, this could enable more systemic reasoning about sector-purpose alignment, compositional risk, and implementation trade-offs, shifting attention from abstract assurances to operationally meaningful claims about how privacy is achieved, under which assumptions and implied residual risks. Developing a library of such artefacts, grounded in real deployments and annotated with metrics and thresholds that matter to decision-makers, would help surface both the enabling and limiting factors of different architectural patterns, supporting more consistent, evidence-driven decisions without prescribing specific tools. 

What an Example ‘Privacy Card’ Could Look Like (High-Level Overview)
A privacy card is a structured, machine-readable artefact that accompanies any PET-protected asset (model, dataset, pipeline, or query service) and records what privacy mechanisms were used, how they were parameterised, what evidence exists, and what residual risks remain. It is not a statement of endorsement, but a conceptual tool for risk assessment and communication.

Key fields might include:
•	Asset identity and version (name, version, owner, date)
•	Threat model and trust assumptions (adversary type, capabilities, trust anchors)
•	Mechanisms used (DP, HE, MPC, FL, TEE, with parameters and toolchain versions)
•	Assurance artefacts (privacy accountant reports, attestation quotes, protocol manifests, audit logs)
•	Utility and accuracy metrics (task, baseline, delta to baseline)
•	Performance profile (latency, throughput, resource cost)
•	Governance mapping (data scope, consent/purpose, release policy, review status)
•	Residual risks and mitigations (known limitations, mitigations applied)
•	Change log (version history, parameter changes)

This card could be generated at each major deployment or release, stored alongside the asset in a registry or data catalogue, and used to support internal review, external audit, or procurement decisions. Over time, a library of privacy cards could serve as cross-sector evidence base for PET adoption, enabling more nuanced, context-sensitive recommendations and risk assessments... 

6. Conclusion
This survey has provided a technical, implementation centred synthesis of PETs. The reviewed literature and deployments demonstrate significant progress and interest in the field alongside persistent challenges related to performance, usability, and assurance. A central finding of this review is that PET effectiveness cannot be inferred from the presence of a mechanism alone. Building on the logic developed in Section 4 and the literature, and real-world examples examined in Sections 2 and 3, we propose that PET viability hinges on how techniques are combined, where trust is placed, and what forms of evidence are generated and communicated to substantiate claims.

The primary contribution of this paper is therefore not a new taxonomy of PETs, but a logic driven reframing of PET adoption as an architectural and evidentiary problem. By organising PETs into algorithmic and architectural typologies and analysing them through shared engineering realities, such as privacy-utility trade offs, composability hazards, integration burdens, and verification requirements, we address a persistent gap in the survey literature. Existing reviews tend to isolate techniques or sectors, rather than cross cutting considerations that transcend any single PET family. The logic model and comparative artefacts developed in this paper make these dependencies explicit, explaining why certain PET configurations recur across sectors, why others remain confined to pilots, and why nominally stronger techniques can fail to deliver meaningful privacy in practice.

Our analysis suggests that further progress in PET adoption will require moving beyond ad hoc evaluations towards more systematic, evidence seeking approaches that capture how privacy guarantees are instantiated in real systems. In particular, the field would benefit from structured representations that encode compositional design choices, trust assumptions, performance characteristics, and residual risks in a form that supports comparison, audit, and reuse across deployments. Although the concrete design, standardisation, and governance of such artefacts remain largely open questions, this idea provides a living framework for context-sensitive privacy engineering, that goes beyond static taxonomies and can evolve alongside PET implementations and sectoral practices. By consolidating recent advances under a shared implementation lens, this survey provides both a snapshot of the current state of the art and a foundation for cumulative, interdisciplinary work aimed at making privacy preserving systems dependable components of modern data infrastructures.
